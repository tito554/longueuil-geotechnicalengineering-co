Electrical Resistivity Surveys (VES) in Longueuil

Together, we solve the challenges of tomorrow.

DISCOVER →

Longueuil’s development from a Seigneurial-era agricultural parish into a dense South Shore hub placed heavy infrastructure on the deep Champlain Sea clay that blankets the region. Anyone who has worked on the Greenfield Park or Vieux-Longueuil benches knows that boreholes alone cannot always resolve the abrupt lateral changes in clay thickness or the variable bedrock topography that influence settlement and excavation stability. Electrical resistivity surveys, carried out as Vertical Electrical Soundings (VES), provide a continuous profile of subsurface resistivity contrasts without the need for extensive intrusive drilling. By injecting a known current through electrode arrays and measuring the resulting potential difference, we map the transition from silty surficial deposits into the conductive marine clays, then into the resistive till and underlying sedimentary bedrock. This non-invasive approach, executed under ASTM D6431-18 guidelines, helps engineers in Longueuil delineate paleochannels, identify pockets of cleaner granular material, and assess groundwater salinity variations — all of which are critical when designing deep foundations near the Saint Lawrence River or planning dewatering for excavations along Taschereau Boulevard, where the soil profile can shift dramatically within a single city block.

Low-resistivity Champlain clay versus high-resistivity till and bedrock creates a stark electrical contrast that makes VES exceptionally effective on the South Shore.

Process overview

In Longueuil, we frequently encounter situations where contractors have drilled a few boreholes along a proposed building footprint and still face uncertainty about the continuity of a buried sand lens or a soft clay seam between the test locations. VES fills that gap by measuring apparent resistivity at progressively deeper investigation depths, generating a 1D layered model that can be correlated with SPT drilling logs to calibrate the geophysical response. A saturated Champlain clay, with its low resistivity of roughly 5 to 30 ohm-m, stands out sharply against a glacial till or a fractured shale bedrock that might read above 100 ohm-m, making VES an efficient tool for mapping the depth to competent bearing strata across an entire site. When the resistivity profile suggests a potential granular layer within the clay sequence, we often recommend targeted CPT testing to confirm the mechanical properties and drainage characteristics of that layer, which can significantly alter the foundation design assumptions. Our field crews use Schlumberger and Wenner array configurations depending on the target depth and site access constraints, processing the data with iterative inversion software to produce a geoelectrical cross-section that is directly usable by the structural and geotechnical engineering teams.

Local context

The field equipment for a VES survey in Longueuil consists of a resistivity meter with a high-impedance voltmeter, stainless steel electrodes, and insulated cable reels that can extend several hundred meters across the terrain. One operational challenge specific to the South Shore is the presence of buried metallic utilities and reinforced concrete foundations in post-industrial zones, which introduce stray currents and short-circuit the electrical field if the array is laid too close. Our crew always performs a utility clearance and, when necessary, shifts to offset arrays or shorter electrode spacing to avoid coupling with underground metal. Another risk factor is the highly conductive nature of the Champlain Sea clay itself: the strong attenuation of the electrical signal with depth can limit the maximum investigation depth unless the transmitted current is increased accordingly. We compensate by using higher-power transmitters and by carefully reducing the contact resistance at each electrode stake, often wetting the ground with a saline solution in very dry summer conditions to ensure reliable current injection and clean data acquisition throughout the sounding sequence.

Additional services

01

1D Vertical Electrical Sounding (VES)

Point-specific resistivity soundings to determine layer thicknesses and resistivity values beneath a single location. Ideal for correlating with existing borehole data and extrapolating the soil profile across a site.

02

2D Electrical Resistivity Tomography (ERT)

Multi-electrode profiling along a linear transect to produce a continuous cross-section of the subsurface resistivity distribution. Applied for mapping lateral variations in clay thickness, detecting paleochannels, and locating the bedrock surface beneath variable overburden.

FAQ

How does VES complement a standard geotechnical investigation in Longueuil?

Standard boreholes provide precise data at discrete points, but the Champlain Sea clay can vary significantly in thickness and composition between boreholes. VES provides continuous subsurface resistivity information that bridges the gaps, helping identify transitions between clay, till, and bedrock, and flagging potential anomalies like buried sand channels that might be missed by a regular drilling grid.

What is the typical depth of investigation for a VES survey on the South Shore?

With Schlumberger arrays and electrode spreads extending up to 200 meters, we can routinely reach investigation depths of 40 to 50 meters, which is sufficient to penetrate the full Champlain Sea sequence and resolve the top of the glacial till or bedrock in most areas of Longueuil. The actual depth is limited by the maximum current output and the conductivity of the near-surface clay.

What is the approximate cost of an electrical resistivity survey in Longueuil?

A VES campaign in Longueuil typically ranges from CA$870 to CA$1,260, depending on the number of soundings, the array length required, site access conditions, and the complexity of data processing and inversion needed to produce the final geoelectrical model.

Can VES be performed in winter when the ground is frozen in Longueuil?

Yes, but frozen ground creates a very high-resistivity surface layer that can impede current injection. We use longer electrode stakes to reach below the frost line and, when necessary, apply a saline solution at the electrode contact points to lower the contact resistance and ensure adequate current flow into the unfrozen soil beneath.
